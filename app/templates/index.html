Project Overview

NASA's Kepler planet finding telescope has found thousands upon thousands of objects of interest by detecting slight dimming in far away stars. Sometimes such dimming indicated the transit of an exoplanet -- a world beyond our solar system -- in front of its host star. Other times it was nothing more than noise in the telescope's signal. Confirming the existence of an exoplanet is a painstaking process -- but could it be automated with machine learning? Doing so would give NASA's researchers more time and resources to study these far off worlds. We at Team_Brute_Force have been exploring NASA's Kepler dataset in order to construct a machine learning algorithm that could tell whether a Kepler Object of Interest (KOI) was more likely to be a true planet or a false positive based on the many columns of data returned by Kepler. We did this by separating the KOI data into two groups -- confirmed planets and false positives -- and then training our algorithm with a neural network to identify patterns that would distinguish the two groups. Our results, as you will see on the 'Exoplanet Detection with Machine Learning' were impressive but it remains to be seen if they are useful. Our secondary mission was to evaluate the thousands of confirmed planets for habitability. You can see the results of this analysis in the 'Data Exploration' section below.

Data Exploration

The above chart depicts all the Kepler confirmed exoplanets (when the planets dataset is toggled) and all their host stars (when the star dataset is toggled) within 200 light years. The colors of the planets indicate temperature with white being the coldest at less than 100 degrees kelvin and red being the hottest at over 500 degrees kelvin. Blue (between 200 and 250 kelvin) and green (between 250 and 300 kelvin) indicate surface temperatures not hostile to life. Our Earth which averages 288 degrees kelvin would appear as green. Of course, temperature is not the only factor in habitability. Click on a planet to view it's habitability index. As you can see from the sample, the vast majority of those nearby exoplanets are too hot to be habitable -- most likely because they orbit very close to their host stars as is indicated by their position on the y-axis. It may be that planets closer to their stars are easier to detect since they would transit more often and thus grant us more chances to observe them. For reference, Neptune travels around the sun once every 165 Earth years while Mercury achieves the same feat nearly 4 times an Earth year.

To find out how many planets in the full dataset were habitable, we created a list of conditions established by the Habitable Exoplanets Catalog at the University of Puerto Rico and then filtered our datafset by those conditions. We were thus able to assign a habitability score from 0 to 2 to each of the 2,620 confirmed exoplanets in the Kepler dataset. A habitability score of 0 meant the planet was uninhabitable with an average temperature of less than 200 Kelvin and a radius of either smaller than half of Earth's radius or larger than 2.5 earth radii. A habitability score of 1 meant that the planet was potentially habitable under optimistic criteria. That is, the planet could be as cool as 200 Kelvin or as hot as 330 Kelvin with a radius as small as half that of earth's or as large as 2.5 earth radii. Finally, the planets with a score of 2 meet more conservative criteria for habitability: they have a radius greater than .5 earth radii and smaller than 1.6 earth radii. As one would expect, the vast majority of Kepler's confirmed exoplanets scored a 0 on habitability. 39 -- or 1.5% -- of the exoplanets met the optimistic criteria for habitability while only 10 -- or .4% -- met the conservative criteria. You can see these results visualized in the chart on the left.

As for the star dataset, you can see that the appearance of the stars matches their actual appearance: the smaller, cooler red dwarves appear as smaller red markers while the larger, hotter main class stars appear as larger, orange and yellow markers. Note that there are no giant stars in this subset of the data. This may be because planet transits are harder to observe in larger, brighter stars.
